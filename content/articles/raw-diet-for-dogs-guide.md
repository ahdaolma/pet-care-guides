## Raw Feeding Works for Wolves — Your Dog Is Not a Wolf

![Raw diet for dogs](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.Feb7hdWze1Fr6eTLmTmFzAHaE8%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=180a4649b9ab12aa19d1b3c2d0004a78d5a8a9dcc401772d28f4b4c782d4e570&ipo=images)

Raw feeding is one of the most passionately debated topics in veterinary nutrition. Proponents cite improved coat condition, cleaner teeth, smaller stools, and "ancestral appropriateness." Opponents — including the FDA, AVMA, and AAHA — cite bacterial contamination risk to humans, nutritional imbalances, and the danger of whole bones.

The truth lies between extremes. Dogs are facultative carnivores — they evolved alongside humans for 15,000-30,000 years eating cooked scraps alongside raw meat, and their digestive systems are more adaptable than obligate carnivores like cats. A properly formulated raw diet CAN be nutritionally complete. An improperly formulated raw diet — which describes the majority of home-prepared raw diets — causes severe deficiencies, particularly in calcium, vitamin D, vitamin E, iodine, and omega-3 fatty acids.

A 2013 study in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association analyzed 200 home-prepared raw dog food recipes from websites and books. Result: 95% were deficient in at least one essential nutrient; 83% were deficient in multiple nutrients. The most common deficiencies: vitamin D, vitamin E, iodine, zinc, and choline. These deficiencies cause no visible symptoms for months or years — then present as bone fractures, thyroid dysfunction, or immune collapse.

If you choose raw feeding, you must work with a veterinary nutritionist. This guide covers the framework — not finished recipes.

## The Two Raw Feeding Models

### Prey Model Raw (PMR): 80-10-10

Mimics the composition of a whole prey animal: 80% muscle meat, 10% edible bone, 10% organs (5% liver + 5% other secreting organ).

**For a 50-lb (23 kg) dog eating 1.5 lbs (680g) daily:**
- Muscle meat: 1.2 lbs (545g) — variety of chicken, turkey, beef, pork, fish
- Edible bone: 0.15 lbs (68g) — raw chicken necks, feet, wing tips. NEVER cooked bone
- Liver: 0.075 lbs (34g) — chicken or beef liver
- Other organ: 0.075 lbs (34g) — kidney, spleen, pancreas, brain

**PMR requires supplementation:** Omega-3 (fish oil, 100 mg/kg EPA/DHA), vitamin E (2 IU per pound of body weight daily), iodine (kelp powder, pinch), and zinc (if not feeding red meat regularly). PMR without supplements is nutritionally incomplete.

### BARF (Biologically Appropriate Raw Food): 70-10-10-10

Adds plant matter: 70% muscle meat, 10% bone, 10% organs, 10% vegetables/fruits/seeds. Created by Dr. Ian Billinghurst in the 1990s.

**Additional BARF components:** Pureed leafy greens (kale, spinach), berries (blueberries, raspberries), pumpkin or sweet potato, ground flaxseed or chia seed (omega-3), and occasionally raw egg (with crushed shell for calcium). The plant matter is pulverized to break cell walls — dogs cannot digest whole plant cells.

## Safety: The FDA and AVMA Position

The FDA warns that raw pet food carries a high risk of contamination with Salmonella, Listeria, Campylobacter, and E. coli. A 2019 FDA study found Salmonella in 15% of tested raw dog foods — approximately 5x the contamination rate of dry kibble. The risk extends to HUMANS: dogs shed these bacteria in feces and saliva for weeks after eating contaminated raw food, even if the dog shows no symptoms. Young children, elderly adults, pregnant women, and immunocompromised individuals are at highest risk.

**Risk mitigation:**
- Handle raw meat with dedicated utensils, cutting board, and preparation area. Sanitize with bleach solution after each use.
- Freeze meat for 2+ weeks before feeding (reduces but does not eliminate some parasites).
- Do not let dogs lick faces or hands after eating raw food.
- Wash hands for 30+ seconds after handling raw food or the dog's bowl.
- Do NOT feed raw if household includes: children under 5, adults over 65, pregnant women, immunocompromised individuals, or anyone undergoing chemotherapy.

## Bones: Which Are Safe and Which Kill

| Safe (RAW only) | Dangerous (RAW) | FATAL (Cooked) |
|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| Chicken necks, feet, wing tips | Weight-bearing bones of large animals (beef femur, pork shank, bison knuckle) — too dense, crack teeth requiring $2,000-$4,000 dental surgery | ALL cooked bones — become brittle, shatter into sharp splinters that perforate the esophagus, stomach, or intestines. | Turkey necks (supervised — choking risk if swallowed whole) | Rib bones (sharp, splinter even raw) | |
| Duck feet, necks | Cut bones (sharp edges from saw cuts) | |
| Rabbit (whole or ground, includes bone) | Bones smaller than the dog's mouth diameter (choking) | |
| Raw meaty bones (RMBs) for recreational chewing — must be LARGER than the dog's head to prevent swallowing whole | | |
