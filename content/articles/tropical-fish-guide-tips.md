## 10 Tips from Experienced Aquarists

### 1. Quarantine Every New Fish — No Exceptions

A single new fish carrying Ich (Ichthyophthirius multifiliis) can wipe out an entire tank in 72 hours. All new fish go into a 10-gallon quarantine tank for 2-4 weeks BEFORE entering the display tank. Quarantine setup: bare bottom (no substrate), heater, sponge filter seeded from main tank, PVC pipe for hiding. Observe for white spots, clamped fins, rapid breathing, or flashing (rubbing against surfaces). Treat prophylactically with Ich-X or aquarium salt at 1 tablespoon per 3 gallons. The quarantine tank costs $40 and saves $200 in dead fish.

### 2. The Python Water Changer Pays for Itself

Bucket water changes are the #1 reason people quit the hobby. A Python water changer ($40-60) connects to your sink faucet and both drains AND fills the tank. A 25% water change on a 55-gallon tank with buckets: 30-45 minutes and a wet floor. With a Python: 10 minutes, no lifting. The single best aquarium purchase after the tank and filter.

### 3. Overfeeding Kills More Fish Than Underfeeding

Fish have stomachs roughly the size of their eyeball. Feed what they eat in 30-60 seconds, once per day. Uneaten food rots, producing ammonia. Skip one day per week (fasting day — fish benefit from digestive rest). The "pinch of flakes 3 times a day" approach is a slow ammonia factory. For bottom feeders, use sinking pellets (Hikari Sinking Wafers). For bettas, 2-3 pellets twice daily.

### 4. The "Mystery Snail" Trap

Mystery snails (Pomacea bridgesii) produce more waste than an equivalent-size fish. A single mystery snail in a 10-gallon tank adds approximately 25% to the bioload. They are cute. They are not "cleanup crew" — they produce massive amounts of ammonia. If you want snails for algae control: Nerite snails (cannot reproduce in freshwater, excellent algae eaters, low bioload).

### 5. Activated Carbon Is Optional (and Often Unnecessary)

Activated carbon removes dissolved organics, medications, and tannins. It does NOT remove ammonia, nitrite, or nitrate. For most tanks, carbon is unnecessary. Replace the carbon slot in your HOB filter with additional biological media (ceramic rings, Matrix, bio-balls). More surface area for bacteria = more stable tank. Carbon is useful after medicating (to remove residual medication) or if you want crystal-clear water. Otherwise, skip it.

### 6. The Aquarium Co-Op Meds Trio

The three medications every aquarist should own before they need them: (1) Ich-X (malachite green + formalin — treats external parasites), (2) Fritz Maracyn (erythromycin — treats gram-positive bacterial infections like fin rot and columnaris), (3) Fritz ParaCleanse (praziquantel + metronidazole — treats internal parasites). Total cost: $30. A fish dying of Ich at 9 PM on a Saturday with no open fish stores is a terrible feeling.

### 7. Your Nitrate Is High Because You Are Not Changing Enough Water

"Help, my nitrate is 80 ppm!" The fix is not more plants or nitrate-removing filter media. It is more water changes. Plants remove 5-10 ppm nitrate per week at most. A 50% water change removes 50% of nitrate immediately. If your nitrate stays above 40 ppm despite weekly 25% changes, increase to 50% weekly. The math is simple. Test your tap water — if it has 20+ ppm nitrate from the source, use RO (reverse osmosis) water or Seachem Purigen.

### 8. Do Not Buy a Common Pleco

Common Plecos (Hypostomus plecostomus) grow to 18-24 inches. They produce more waste than any other freshwater fish. They stop eating algae as adults and eat fish food instead. They destroy planted tanks by uprooting everything. Pet stores sell them as "algae eaters" at 2 inches to uninformed beginners. Bristlenose Plecos (Ancistrus species) stay at 4-5 inches and actually eat algae their entire lives. The Common Pleco is the most irresponsibly sold fish in the hobby.

### 9. Live Plants = Free Filtration

Fast-growing stem plants (Hornwort, Water Wisteria, Anacharis, Water Sprite) absorb ammonia, nitrite, AND nitrate directly from the water column. A heavily planted 20-gallon tank with fast-growing stems may only need water changes every 2-3 weeks. Floating plants (Duckweed, Salvinia, Amazon Frogbit) are nitrate-absorbing machines. Start with Anubias and Java Fern — nearly unkillable, attach to driftwood or rocks, no special substrate needed.

### 10. The Aquarium Co-Op Heater Controller

Heaters fail. When they fail in the "ON" position (the most common failure mode), they cook your fish at 95°F+. An Inkbird temperature controller ($35) sits between the heater and the wall outlet. Set the heater to 82°F, set the controller to 78°F. If the heater thermostat fails, the controller cuts power at 78°F. This $35 device has saved thousands of tanks. Second most important safety device after a lid (prevents jumpers).

## Key Takeaway

The difference between a beautiful aquarium and a fish graveyard is preparation. Quarantine new fish. Own medications before you need them. Use a heater controller. Do water changes with a Python. And never, ever buy a Common Pleco.
