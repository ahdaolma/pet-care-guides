## 10 Pro Tips for Traveling with Your Pet

### 1. Update the Microchip BEFORE You Leave

The #1 reason lost pets are not reunited with their owners: the microchip is registered to an old phone number. Log into the microchip database (HomeAgain, AKC Reunite, 24PetWatch, or Found Animals) and confirm your cell phone number is current. The phone number on the tag should be YOUR cell — not your home landline that you will not be near for the next week.

### 2. Feed 3 Hours Before Departure

A full stomach + motion sickness = vomit in your car or carrier. Feed breakfast, then wait 3 hours before driving. Small amounts of water are fine. For dogs prone to motion sickness: Cerenia (maropitant, prescription from vet) given 2 hours before travel prevents vomiting with 95%+ efficacy in clinical trials. Do not use human Dramamine without veterinary dosing instructions.

### 3. The 5-Minute Hotel Room Setup

The moment you enter a hotel room: (1) Set up the crate or dog bed in a corner with a familiar blanket. (2) Put down the water bowl. (3) Walk the dog around the room on-leash for 2 minutes so they smell everything. (4) Give a frozen Kong or chew. This routine tells the dog "this is our temporary den" instead of "this is a strange scary place where I must patrol and bark."

### 4. Never Leave a Dog Loose in a Hotel Room

A loose dog in a hotel room will: bark at hallway sounds, scratch the door, chew furniture, and possibly escape when housekeeping enters. Always crate. Put the "Do Not Disturb" sign on the door. If housekeeping must enter, you are present or the dog is with you. Housekeepers open doors quickly and a spooked dog bolts.

### 5. The Rabies Certificate Lives in Your Glove Box

For road trips across state lines: keep a paper copy of the rabies vaccination certificate in the glove box. Some states (particularly in the South and Midwest) have agricultural checkpoints at state borders that can request proof of rabies vaccination. A digital photo is rarely accepted. Paper is king.

### 6. Book Pet-Friendly Hotels Directly

Third-party booking sites (Expedia, Booking.com) often display "pet friendly" inaccurately. Call the hotel directly and ask: "What is your pet fee, weight limit, and are there breed restrictions?" Then book directly. This also gives you a human contact if something goes wrong. HotelTonight and other last-minute apps are unreliable for pet travel.

### 7. Pack Your Dog's Water from Home

Different municipal water supplies have different mineral contents, chlorine levels, and bacteria. Switching water suddenly causes GI upset in sensitive dogs — diarrhea on a road trip is a special kind of misery. Bring a gallon of your home tap water per 2 days of travel. Refill at the destination if you will be there more than a few days.

### 8. Airline Carrier Training Starts 4 Weeks Before the Flight

Do not buy a carrier the day before and expect your dog or cat to tolerate 6 hours in it. Start 4 weeks before: leave the carrier open in the living room with treats inside. Feed meals in the carrier. Close the door for 30 seconds → 1 minute → 5 minutes → 30 minutes → 2 hours. The goal: the carrier is a safe place, not a punishment box. For cats, this is especially critical — a cat that panics in a carrier at TSA will get loose and may be lost in the airport.

### 9. The Health Certificate Is Valid 10 Days — Plan Return Accordingly

The CVI (Certificate of Veterinary Inspection) is valid for 10 days from the date of issue for most states. If your trip is longer than 10 days, you need either: (A) a new CVI from a vet at your destination for the return trip, or (B) to return before the 10-day window closes. International CVIs have different validity periods — check the USDA APHIS pet travel website for your destination country.

### 10. Know the Nearest Emergency Vet at Your Destination

Before you leave, Google "24-hour emergency veterinarian near [hotel address]" and save the number in your phone. Write down the address. If your dog eats something toxic at 2 AM in a strange city, you do not want to be searching for a vet while panicking. Many emergency vets have 4-6 hour waits — calling ahead saves critical time.

## Key Takeaway

Pet travel fails in the details — the expired microchip registration, the denied hotel reservation, the missing rabies certificate at the state border. Spend 30 minutes on preparation and save yourself a ruined trip.
