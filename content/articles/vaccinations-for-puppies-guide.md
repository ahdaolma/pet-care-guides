## Your Puppy's Immune System Is a Blank Slate — Vaccines Are the Training Wheels

![Puppy vaccination](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse2.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.QgkFDrfqQSTySQj26t48HwHaHr%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=698a3e46e30b056f711c1435a4860a26693e666ec10301487c66e929ded048ed&ipo=images)

Puppies receive maternal antibodies from their mother's colostrum (first milk) in the first 24 hours of life. These antibodies protect them — but they also interfere with vaccines. The maternal antibodies wane between 6-16 weeks. Vaccines given while maternal antibodies are still high are neutralized and provide zero protection. Vaccines given after they wane leave the puppy unprotected during the "window of susceptibility." This is why puppies need a SERIES of shots, not a single vaccine.

---

## Core vs Non-Core Vaccines

**Core vaccines (ALL puppies need):** DHPP (Distemper, Hepatitis/Adenovirus-2, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza) and Rabies. These diseases are universally present, highly contagious, and often fatal.

**Non-core vaccines (based on lifestyle/geography):** Bordetella (kennel cough), Leptospirosis, Lyme disease, Canine Influenza (H3N2/H3N8), and Rattlesnake vaccine. Your veterinarian will recommend these based on where you live and what your dog does.

## The 2026 AAHA Vaccination Schedule

The American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA) publishes the definitive canine vaccination guidelines. Here is the 2026 recommended schedule:

| Age | Core Vaccines | Non-Core (if recommended) | Notes |
|-----|--------------|--------------------------|-------|
| 6-8 weeks | DHPP #1 | Bordetella (intranasal or oral) | First vet visit. Physical exam + fecal test for parasites. |
| 10-12 weeks | DHPP #2 | Leptospirosis #1, Lyme #1, Canine Influenza #1 | Second vet visit. |
| 14-16 weeks | DHPP #3 (final booster in series) | Lepto #2, Lyme #2, Canine Influenza #2 | THIS is the critical shot. Maternal antibodies should be gone. |
| 16-18 weeks | Rabies (1-year) | — | Required by law in all 50 states. Must be administered by a veterinarian. |
| 1 year | DHPP booster, Rabies (1-year or 3-year) | Lepto booster, Lyme booster, Bordetella booster | The "1-year checkup." Booster DHPP and rabies. |
| Every 1-3 years thereafter | DHPP (1 or 3 year), Rabies (1 or 3 year) | Lepto (annual), Lyme (annual), Bordetella (6-12 months) | Titer testing is an alternative to automatic boosters for DHPP. |

**Critical note:** The final DHPP booster MUST be given at or after 16 weeks. Vaccines given at 12-14 weeks may still be neutralized by lingering maternal antibodies in some puppies. The 16-week shot is the one that guarantees immunity. Do not let your vet skip the 16-week visit.

## What Each Vaccine Prevents

### DHPP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parvo, Parainfluenza)
- **Distemper:** A virus that attacks the respiratory, GI, and nervous systems. Mortality rate: 50% in adults, 80% in puppies. Survivors often have permanent neurological damage. Spread by airborne droplets.
- **Canine Adenovirus-2 (Hepatitis):** Causes infectious canine hepatitis — liver failure, corneal edema ("blue eye"), and death. CAV-2 vaccine also cross-protects against CAV-1.
- **Parvovirus:** The one every puppy owner fears. Attacks rapidly dividing cells in the intestinal lining and bone marrow. Symptoms: severe bloody diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration, sepsis. Without treatment: 91% mortality. With aggressive ICU care: 80-90% survival — at a cost of $3,000-8,000. Parvo lives in the environment for YEARS. Unvaccinated puppies should not walk on public ground until 2 weeks after their final DHPP booster.
- **Parainfluenza:** One of the pathogens in the "kennel cough" complex. Causes upper respiratory infection. Not typically fatal but makes puppies miserable.

### Rabies
A virus that attacks the central nervous system. 100% fatal once symptoms appear — in dogs AND humans. Transmitted through saliva (bites). Required by law. First vaccine is valid for 1 year. Subsequent vaccines may be 3-year depending on the product used and state law.

### Leptospirosis (Non-Core but Increasingly Recommended)
A bacterial disease spread through the urine of infected wildlife (rats, raccoons, deer). Dogs contract it by drinking from puddles, ponds, or any standing water contaminated with infected urine. Causes acute kidney and liver failure. ZOONOTIC — humans can catch it from their infected dog's urine. The old Lepto vaccines (pre-2015) had a reputation for allergic reactions. Modern Lepto vaccines (Nobivac Lepto4, Vanguard L4) are purified subunit vaccines with dramatically fewer reactions. Many vets now consider Lepto a core vaccine in most of the US.

### Bordetella (Kennel Cough)
A bacterial component of the kennel cough complex. Required by virtually all boarding facilities, daycares, and groomers. Available as intranasal (faster onset, localized immunity), oral, or injectable. The intranasal form provides immunity in 48-72 hours and is preferred. Protection lasts 6-12 months.

### Lyme Disease (Borrelia burgdorferi)
Tick-borne. Endemic in the Northeast, Upper Midwest, and Pacific Coast. Causes shifting-leg lameness, fever, and in chronic cases, kidney failure (Lyme nephritis — often fatal). Only recommended in endemic areas AND for dogs with tick exposure. The vaccine is not 100% effective — tick prevention (NexGard, Simparica, Bravecto) is equally important.

### Canine Influenza (H3N2/H3N8)
"Dog flu." Two strains. Highly contagious in kennels, shelters, and daycares. Causes cough, fever, nasal discharge — indistinguishable from kennel cough but more severe. Mortality is low (<5%) but morbidity is high. The vaccine reduces severity but does not prevent infection entirely. Recommended for dogs that board, attend daycare, or go to dog shows.

## The Small-Breed Vaccine Risk

Small-breed dogs (under 10 lbs) and toy breeds (Chihuahuas, Yorkies, Toy Poodles) have a higher risk of vaccine reactions — facial swelling, hives, vomiting, and in very rare cases, anaphylaxis. The risk is approximately 1 in 250 doses for small breeds vs 1 in 2,500 for large breeds, according to a 2024 veterinary pharmacovigilance study.

The AAHA recommendation: for small-breed puppies, split the Lepto and Lyme vaccines from the DHPP booster — give them at separate visits (2 weeks apart). Do not administer DHPP + Lepto + Lyme + Bordetella + Rabies all at the same visit in a 4-pound Chihuahua. Space them out.

## Key Takeaway

Your puppy needs DHPP at 8, 12, and 16 weeks minimum. The 16-week shot is non-negotiable. Rabies at 16-18 weeks is legally required. Non-core vaccines (Lepto, Lyme, Bordetella) depend on your location and lifestyle. Space vaccines 2+ weeks apart for small breeds. And keep unvaccinated puppies off public ground until 2 weeks after the final DHPP — parvo is everywhere.
