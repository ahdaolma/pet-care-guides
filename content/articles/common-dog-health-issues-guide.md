## Your Dog Cannot Tell You It Hurts — Learn the Signs

![Common dog health issues](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthf.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.RAuwcowX40b-9OonLv3j6wHaLH%3Fr%3D0%26cb%3Dthfc1falcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=2a144b4f779e9849df54bf28ee52adb5b93979a67a5cf13913618a7fb686d155&ipo=images)

Dogs are descended from wolves and retain the survival instinct to hide pain. In a pack, showing weakness means falling in the hierarchy or becoming a target. By the time a dog visibly limps, cries, or stops eating, the underlying condition has often been progressing for weeks or months.

According to the American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA), the top reasons for canine veterinary visits in 2024 were: ear infections (otitis externa), skin allergies (atopic dermatitis), dental disease, arthritis/hip dysplasia, and gastrointestinal issues. Together, these five conditions account for approximately 55% of all non-routine veterinary visits for dogs.

This guide covers the 10 most common conditions, their early warning signs (before the dog is visibly sick), prevention strategies, and the costs of treatment vs. prevention.

---

## 1. Ear Infections (Otitis Externa) — The #1 Vet Visit Cause

**Prevalence:** 15-20% of dogs will develop an ear infection in any given year. Breeds with floppy ears (Cocker Spaniels, Basset Hounds, Golden Retrievers, Labradoodles) and dogs that swim frequently have 3-5x higher risk.

**What it is:** Inflammation and infection of the external ear canal, usually caused by bacteria (Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus), yeast (Malassezia), or both. The warm, dark, moist environment of a floppy-eared dog's ear canal is a perfect incubator.

**Early signs (before the dog is in obvious pain):**
- Shaking head more than usual (more than 2-3 head shakes per hour is abnormal)
- Scratching at one ear specifically
- Ear smells slightly "yeasty" or sour, like bread dough or cheese
- Slight redness visible at the ear opening
- Dark brown or yellow discharge, even a small amount

**Advanced signs (infection is established):**
- Ears are red, swollen, and painful to touch
- Thick, dark, foul-smelling discharge
- Dog cries or snaps when ears are touched
- Head tilted to one side (may indicate middle ear involvement)
- Loss of balance, walking in circles (inner ear involvement — emergency)

**Vet treatment:** Cytology (ear swab examined under microscope to identify organism, $30-$50), ear cleaning under sedation if painful ($50-$100), topical medication (antibiotic/antifungal/steroid drops, $30-$60 per bottle, 7-14 day course). Total: $150-$300 for an uncomplicated infection. Recurrent or resistant infections requiring culture and systemic antibiotics: $300-$800.

**Prevention:** Weekly ear checks. For floppy-eared or swimming dogs: clean ears with a veterinary ear cleaner (Epi-Otic, $15) after every swim or bath. Squeeze cleaner into the ear canal, massage the base of the ear for 30 seconds (you should hear a squelching sound), then let the dog shake. Wipe the outer ear with a cotton ball — never push anything INTO the ear canal (Q-tips push debris deeper and can rupture the eardrum).

## 2. Skin Allergies (Atopic Dermatitis) — Dogs' Most Frustrating Chronic Condition

**Prevalence:** Affects 10-15% of dogs. Certain breeds (West Highland White Terriers, French Bulldogs, Golden Retrievers, Labrador Retrievers, German Shepherds, Boxers) have genetic predisposition.

**What it is:** An overactive immune response to environmental allergens (pollen, dust mites, mold), food proteins, or flea saliva. The allergic reaction manifests in the SKIN, not the respiratory system (unlike humans who get hay fever).

**Early signs:**
- Licking paws excessively (especially between toes — visible rust-colored staining on light-colored fur is from saliva porphyrins)
- Rubbing face on carpet or furniture
- Recurrent ear infections (allergy and ear infections often cycle together)
- Skin feels greasy or smells yeasty
- Hair loss in symmetrical patterns on flanks, belly, or inner thighs

**Advanced signs:**
- Hot spots (acute moist dermatitis — red, oozing, hairless patches that appear within hours)
- Chronically thickened, darkened skin (lichenification, "elephant skin")
- Secondary bacterial or yeast skin infections (pyoderma)
- Year-round symptoms (initially may be seasonal)

**Vet treatment:** Elimination diet trial to rule out food allergies (prescription hydrolyzed protein diet exclusively for 8-12 weeks, $80-$120/month), intradermal skin testing or blood allergy testing ($200-$500), and management with one or more of:

| Treatment | Mechanism | Monthly Cost | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-------|
| Apoquel (oral) | Blocks itch signal (JAK inhibitor) | $60-$120 | Rapid relief (within 4 hours). Daily pill. Safe long-term. |
| Cytopoint (injection) | Antibody that neutralizes itch signal (IL-31) | $50-$120 | Given at vet every 4-8 weeks. 80% response rate. |
| Immunotherapy (allergy shots or sublingual drops) | Desensitizes immune system to specific allergens | $100-$200 initial, $30-$60/month maintenance | Only treatment addressing root cause. Takes 6-12 months for full effect. |
| Medicated shampoo (antifungal/antibacterial) | Controls secondary infections | $15-$25/month | Bathe 1-2x weekly during flares. |

Untreated chronic allergies cost far more in secondary infections, ear disease, and reduced quality of life. Most allergic dogs require lifelong management, not a "cure."

## 3. Dental Disease (Periodontal Disease) — Affects 80%+ of Dogs by Age 3

**Prevalence:** The American Veterinary Dental College states that 80% of dogs have some degree of periodontal disease by age 3. Small and toy breeds (Yorkies, Chihuahuas, Dachshunds) are disproportionately affected due to tooth crowding in small jaws.

**What it is:** Bacterial plaque hardens into tartar (calculus), which migrates below the gum line, destroying the periodontal ligament and jawbone that anchor the teeth.

**Early signs:**
- Bad breath (halitosis — that classic "dog breath" is actually the smell of bacterial waste products)
- Yellow-brown tartar visible on back molars
- Red line along the gum margin (gingivitis — fully reversible at this stage)

**Advanced signs:**
- Bleeding gums
- Loose or missing teeth
- Receding gums exposing tooth roots
- Reluctance to eat hard food or chew toys
- Swelling under the eye (tooth root abscess)
- Systemic effects: bacteria from the mouth enter the bloodstream and damage heart valves (endocarditis), kidneys, and liver

**Treatment:** Professional dental cleaning under anesthesia ($400-$1,200 depending on severity, extractions, and geographic region). Anesthesia-free dentistry (offered by some groomers) is cosmetic only — subgingival disease that causes organ damage cannot be addressed without anesthesia. The AVDC position statement: "anesthesia-free dentistry is below the standard of care."

**Prevention:** Daily brushing with dog toothpaste (enzymatic — CET, Virbac, $8-$12 per tube). VOHC-approved dental chews (Greenies, Oravet, Purina DentaLife). Prescription dental diet (Hill's t/d) for dogs that will not tolerate brushing. Annual veterinary oral exam.

## 4. Hip Dysplasia — The Silent Degeneration

**Prevalence:** Affects 15-20% of large and giant breed dogs. Golden Retrievers (20-25% in some lines), German Shepherds (19%), Rottweilers (20%), Labrador Retrievers (12%), and Bulldogs (70%+ — the highest rate of any breed). OFA (Orthopedic Foundation for Animals) maintains a database of hip scores for breeding dogs.

**What it is:** A malformation of the hip joint where the femoral head (ball) does not fit snugly into the acetabulum (socket). This laxity causes abnormal wear, cartilage erosion, and ultimately arthritis. It is polygenic (multiple genes) + environmental (rapid growth, excess weight, inappropriate exercise during development).

**Early signs (often missed for years):**
- "Bunny hopping" — running with both hind legs moving together instead of alternating
- Difficulty rising from lying down (especially in the morning or after rest)
- Reluctance to jump into the car or onto furniture
- Swaying gait (hips sway side to side when walking)
- Sitting with one leg splayed out to the side ("lazy sit")
- Muscle atrophy in the hindquarters (thighs look thin compared to chest/shoulders)

**Advanced signs:**
- Obvious limping on one or both hind legs
- Audible clicking or grinding when the hip is moved (crepitus — bone on bone)
- Unable to climb stairs
- Irritability when hindquarters are touched
- Significantly reduced activity level

**Diagnosis:** Hip X-rays under sedation (PennHIP method is more predictive than OFA for puppies as young as 16 weeks, $300-$500). For adult dogs with symptoms, standard hip radiographs confirm the diagnosis and assess arthritis severity.

**Treatment:**
- **Conservative (mild-moderate):** Weight management (every pound of excess weight adds 4 pounds of force on the hip joint), joint supplements (omega-3 fatty acids from fish oil at 100 mg/kg EPA/DHA, glucosamine/chondroitin — Dasuquin Advanced, $40/month), NSAIDs for pain (carprofen/Rimadyl, $20-$50/month generic), physical therapy and controlled low-impact exercise (swimming, walking on soft surfaces), Adequan injections ($50-$80 per injection, series of 8, then monthly maintenance).
- **Surgical (severe):** Total hip replacement ($5,000-$7,000 per hip) — gold standard with 90-95% success rate. Femoral head ostectomy (FHO, $1,500-$2,500) — salvages function in smaller dogs (<50 lbs) by removing the femoral head entirely and allowing scar tissue to form a "false joint."

## 5. Arthritis (Osteoarthritis) — Not a Disease of Old Age Alone

**Prevalence:** Affects 20-25% of all dogs, and 60-80% of dogs over 8 years old. Any dog with a prior joint injury or structural abnormality (hip/elbow dysplasia, luxating patella, cruciate ligament tear) will develop arthritis in that joint, often years before "old age."

**Early signs (often attributed to "just getting older" — do not accept this):**
- Stiffness when first getting up that improves after movement
- Lagging behind on walks that were previously easy
- Hesitating before stairs
- Less enthusiasm for play
- Sleeping more, interacting less
- Licking at specific joints (the warmth and moisture provides temporary relief)

**Management:** This is the same as hip dysplasia conservative management above. The earlier intervention starts, the longer function and comfort are preserved. Arthritis cannot be cured, but progression can be slowed by 30-50% with consistent multimodal management.

## 6-10: Rapid Reference

### 6. Obesity — 56% of US Dogs Are Overweight (APOP 2024 Data)
**Consequences:** Reduces lifespan by 2 years on average (Purina lifespan study). Doubles risk of cruciate ligament tears, triples risk of arthritis, quadruples risk of diabetes in predisposed breeds. **Fix:** Measure food by weight (grams), not volume (cups — wildly inaccurate). Body condition score: you should feel ribs with light pressure (like the back of your hand), see a visible waist from above, and see an abdominal tuck from the side.

### 7. GI Issues (Vomiting/Diarrhea) — The #1 Acute Emergency Visit Cause
**Most common:** Dietary indiscretion (ate something gross), sudden diet change (transition new food over 7-10 days by mixing increasing proportions), pancreatitis (fatty meal triggers enzyme release that digests the pancreas — 40% mortality in severe cases), intestinal parasites (Giardia, roundworms, hookworms). **Cost:** $200-$500 for mild gastroenteritis, $1,500-$4,000 for pancreatitis requiring hospitalization.

### 8. Cruciate Ligament Tear (ACL Equivalent)
**Affects:** Overweight large breeds, especially Labrador Retrievers and Rottweilers. **Sign:** Sudden non-weight-bearing lameness after running/turning — the dog holds the leg completely off the ground, toe just touching. **Cost:** TPLO surgery $3,000-$5,500 per knee. 40-60% of dogs tear the opposite knee within 2 years. **Prevention:** Weight control is the single most protective factor.

### 9. Hypothyroidism — The "Slowing Down" Disease
**Affects:** Middle-aged medium-to-large breeds (Golden Retrievers, Dobermans, Boxers, Cocker Spaniels). **Signs:** Weight gain without increased food, lethargy, hair loss (symmetrical, "rat tail"), recurrent skin infections, cold intolerance. **Diagnosis:** Blood test (T4, free T4, TSH), $80-$150. **Treatment:** Synthetic thyroxine (levothyroxine), $15-$30/month generic. Lifelong but easily managed with 6-month blood level checks.

### 10. Cancer — The Leading Cause of Death in Dogs Over 10
**Prevalence:** 1 in 4 dogs will develop cancer in their lifetime (AVMA). 50% of dogs over 10 die of cancer. Most common: lymphoma, mast cell tumors, osteosarcoma (bone cancer in large breeds), hemangiosarcoma (spleen/heart tumor in Golden Retrievers and German Shepherds). **Early detection saves lives:** any lump that grows, changes texture, or persists longer than 2 weeks needs a fine needle aspirate ($100-$200 at the vet). Do not "watch and wait" with lumps.
