## A Tired Dog Is a Good Dog — But "Tired" Means Different Things for Different Breeds

![Dog exercise needs by breed](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.-xnabjODTrAjgIZ90eWrbAHaMW%3Fpid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=374ab27e775ef1cabf703089b70b4a9fe23df807c0810778ed37d4d84b77c6a7&ipo=images)

According to the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention (APOP), 59% of dogs in the US were classified as overweight or obese in 2024. The American Veterinary Society of Animal Behavior (AVSAB) identifies inadequate exercise as a contributing factor in approximately 40% of canine behavioral problems presented to veterinary behaviorists — including destructive chewing, excessive barking, hyperactivity, and aggression.

But "exercise" is not one-size-fits-all. A 30-minute walk that leaves a Basset Hound ready for a 4-hour nap barely registers for a Border Collie. The mismatch between owner expectations ("I walk my Husky 20 minutes a day") and breed requirements ("Huskies were bred to run 100+ miles per day pulling sleds") is the underlying cause of millions of surrendered dogs.

This guide breaks down exercise needs by breed group, with specific numbers in minutes per day — not vague recommendations like "needs plenty of exercise."

---

## The Two Types of Exercise: Physical vs. Mental

Before diving into breed-specific numbers, it is essential to understand that dogs need BOTH physical and mental exercise. Physical exercise alone — even intense running — does not fully satisfy a working breed. A Border Collie that runs 5 miles but does no mental work is a physically tired dog with a still-racing brain — and that brain will find a "job" whether you assign one or not.

| Exercise Type | What It Is | Examples | Signs Your Dog Needs More of This |
|--------------|-----------|----------|----------------------------------|
| **Physical** | Aerobic activity that raises heart rate and works muscles | Running, swimming, fetch, hiking, flirt pole, dog sports (agility, flyball) | Destructive chewing, hyperactivity, weight gain, restlessness at night |
| **Mental** | Problem-solving, sniffing, learning, and impulse control | Puzzle feeders, nose work, trick training, obedience drills, sniff walks ("sniffaris"), hide-and-seek | Barking at nothing, digging, pacing, chasing shadows/lights, obsessive behaviors |

**The golden ratio:** For high-drive working and herding breeds, aim for roughly 60% physical + 40% mental exercise. For scent hounds, flip it — 40% physical + 60% mental (sniffing is their primary need). For companion breeds, 50/50 works well.

15 minutes of sniffing is as mentally tiring as 60 minutes of walking. A "sniff walk" — where you let the dog choose the pace and direction, stopping to sniff everything — is not "wasting time." It is providing the mental equivalent of a hard run.

## Exercise Requirements by Breed Group

### Sporting Group (Labradors, Golden Retrievers, Spaniels, Pointers, Vizslas, Weimaraners)

**Daily requirement:** 60-90 minutes of active exercise (elevated heart rate — walking at a human pace is insufficient, you need to be jogging or the dog needs to be running off-leash or playing fetch).

**What "enough" looks like:** A 30-minute off-leash run or fetch session (the dog sprints, not jogs) + a 30-minute walk or training session. Swimming counts and is easier on joints — 20 minutes of swimming equals roughly 60 minutes of running for cardiovascular benefit.

**Mental work needed:** Moderate. Sporting breeds are cooperative and want to please, so 10-15 minutes of training (obedience, retrieving drills, trick training) satisfies their mental needs.

**Under-exercised sporting dog warning signs:** Labrador eats the drywall. Golden Retriever steals and destroys shoes/socks/remote controls. Vizsla develops separation anxiety and vocalizes for hours. Weimaraner becomes neurotic and hyperactive.

**Bottom line:** These are the most popular family dogs because they are tolerant and trainable — but they are athletes. A "family Lab" is still a Lab. Without daily off-leash running or equivalent, these dogs become destructive.

---

### Herding Group (Border Collies, Australian Shepherds, German Shepherds, Corgis, Malinois, Shetland Sheepdogs)

**Daily requirement:** 90-120 minutes of combined physical + mental exercise. This is not optional — it is the minimum for a manageable dog.

**What "enough" looks like:** 60 minutes of intense physical exercise (running, fetch, agility, herding — walking does not count for this group) + 30+ minutes of structured mental work (obedience training, trick training, nose work, puzzle solving, or a dog sport like agility or rally).

**The mental work is NOT OPTIONAL.** A Border Collie run for 2 hours but given no mental work will invent its own mental stimulation: herding children by nipping heels, chasing shadows/reflections into obsessive-compulsive disorder, dismantling furniture with surgical precision, or "patrolling" the fence line for 8 hours barking at everything.

**The 15-minute rule for herding breeds:** If you cannot provide at least 90 minutes of combined exercise, do not get a herding breed. If you can provide 60 minutes but not 90, consider a senior herding dog (8+ years) or a low-drive individual from a rescue — but know that you are the exception, not the rule, and you are gambling with the dog's wellbeing.

**Under-exercised herding dog warning signs:** Nipping at children's heels (genetically programmed herding behavior, triggered by running/squealing), obsessive behaviors (chasing shadows, lights, water droplets), reactivity toward moving objects (cars, bikes, joggers), destruction of walls/floors/doors when left alone.

---

### Working Group (Huskies, Malamutes, Rottweilers, Dobermans, Boxers, Great Danes, Mastiffs, Akitas, Bernese Mountain Dogs)

The working group has the widest variation. Sled dogs (Huskies, Malamutes) are essentially wolves in terms of energy: 90-120 minutes of RUNNING daily. Guardian breeds (Mastiffs, Great Danes) are surprisingly moderate: 30-45 minutes of walking.

| Breed | Daily Exercise | Type | Notes |
|-------|---------------|------|-------|
| Husky, Malamute | 90-120 min | Running, pulling (bikejoring, canicross), hiking. Walking does NOT tire a Husky. | Escape artists. Dig under or climb over 6-foot fences. Off-leash unreliable — recall is 0% when running. |
| Rottweiler, Doberman | 60-90 min | Running, structured training, protection sports | Need a JOB — not just exercise. Obedience and bite work satisfy the working drive. |
| Boxer | 60-75 min | Running, fetch, play with other dogs | "Forever puppy" mentality until age 4-5. Goofy and high-energy. |
| Great Dane, Mastiff, Newfoundland | 30-45 min | Walking, short play sessions | Giant breeds — exercise caution on joints during growth (under 18 months). No forced running on hard surfaces during development. |
| Bernese Mountain Dog | 45-60 min | Hiking, walking, pulling cart (they were bred to pull milk carts) | Shorter lifespan (6-8 years). Moderate energy. Love cold weather — overheat above 75°F. |

**Critical note on giant breeds and puppies:** Large and giant breed puppies should NOT do forced running (jogging alongside a human, running on pavement) until growth plates close (12-18 months for large breeds, 18-24 months for giants). Premature high-impact exercise causes joint damage that manifests as arthritis years earlier. Free play (the puppy chooses to run and can stop) on soft surfaces (grass, sand) is safe and encouraged.

### Terrier Group (Jack Russell, Rat Terrier, Airedale, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, Westie, Cairn)

**Daily requirement:** 45-60 minutes of active exercise + 15-20 minutes of mental work. The size-to-energy ratio is the most misleading in the dog world — a 15-pound Jack Russell needs more exercise than a 70-pound Labrador.

**What "enough" looks like:** A terrier needs to RUN, DIG, and SHRED. A walk around the block is a warm-up. Add a flirt pole session (10-15 minutes of chasing, jumping, and tugging — exhausts a terrier faster than anything else), a digging box (sandbox or kiddie pool filled with dirt/sand with buried toys), and shredding toys (cardboard boxes to destroy — supervised, not ingested).

**Under-exercised terrier warning signs:** Barking at everything (boredom barking becomes self-reinforcing), digging through carpet/flooring (redirecting the dig instinct onto your house), aggression toward other dogs (frustration from pent-up energy), escaping (terriers find ways out of secure yards when under-stimulated).

### Hound Group

**Sighthounds (Greyhounds, Whippets, Italian Greyhounds, Borzoi):**
**Daily requirement:** Surprisingly LOW. 20-30 minutes of full-speed sprinting (in a securely fenced area ONLY — recall is nonexistent at 40+ mph), then 22+ hours of couch. Retired racing Greyhounds are the ultimate apartment dogs — they are 45-mph couch potatoes. Whippets and Italian Greyhounds need slightly more: 30-40 minutes. Do not mistake their laziness for low exercise tolerance — they still need the brief sprint outlet. Without it, they become restless and may develop destructive habits.

**Scenthounds (Beagles, Basset Hounds, Bloodhounds, Dachshunds, Coonhounds):**
**Daily requirement:** 45-60 minutes, but the TYPE matters more than the duration. A scent hound on a 60-minute "sniff walk" (covering 1 mile, nose to the ground the entire time) is more satisfied than a scent hound forced to heel-jog for 3 miles. Their exercise need is primarily olfactory — they need to SNIFF. Off-leash is not recommended for scenthounds — a Beagle on a scent trail is deaf to recall and will follow its nose into traffic.

---

### Toy and Companion Breeds (Chihuahuas, Shih Tzus, Maltese, Cavaliers, Bichons, Pugs)

**Daily requirement:** 20-40 minutes of walking and indoor play. These dogs were bred for companionship, not work. Their exercise needs are modest and can be met with a 20-minute walk + 10-15 minutes of indoor play/training.

**Important caveats for brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds:** Pugs, French Bulldogs, English Bulldogs, and Boston Terriers have structurally compromised airways. They cannot pant efficiently, which means they cannot cool themselves during exercise. Do NOT exercise these breeds when the temperature exceeds 75°F. Early morning or late evening only in summer. Watch for: loud snorting that intensifies during exercise, gums turning pale or blue (oxygen deprivation — EMERGENCY), or the dog lying down and refusing to move (not stubbornness — it cannot breathe). Many brachycephalic dogs need airway surgery (BOAS surgery, $2,000-$5,000) to exercise comfortably.

## Signs Your Dog Is Under-Exercised (and Over-Exercised)

### Under-Exercised
- Destructive behavior (chewing furniture, digging indoors, destroying belongings) — especially when left alone
- Hyperactivity — cannot settle, paces constantly, "zoomies" multiple times daily in adult dogs
- Excessive barking or whining
- Attention-seeking behavior (nudging, pawing, stealing items to provoke a chase)
- Weight gain despite appropriate feeding
- Restlessness at night — the dog paces or cannot settle when the household is sleeping

### Over-Exercised
- Limping or stiffness during or after exercise (joint pain — not "tiredness")
- Reluctance to continue — the dog lies down, lags behind, or refuses to move forward (NOT stubbornness)
- Excessive panting that continues more than 10 minutes after exercise stops
- Paw pads that are raw, blistered, or bleeding (especially on hot pavement — if you cannot hold the back of your hand on the pavement for 7 seconds, it is too hot for paw pads)
- Soreness the next day — reluctance to rise, stiffness, decreased activity
- Dark urine (myoglobinuria — muscle breakdown products in urine, can cause kidney damage — EMERGENCY)

## For Busy Owners: Alternatives to the 60-Minute Walk

You work 10 hours. You have a high-energy dog. Here is how to make it work:

| Alternative | Time Required | Effectiveness | Cost |
|------------|--------------|--------------|------|
| Doggy daycare (1-2x/week) | Drop-off/pickup | Excellent — social play + running all day | $25-$45 per day |
| Midday dog walker (daily) | 0 min | Good — 30-min walk breaks up the day | $15-$25 per walk ($300-$500/month) |
| Flirt pole session (before work) | 15 min | Excellent — 15 min of flirt pole = 45 min of walking for high-drive dogs | $15 one-time (Outward Hound flirt pole) |
| Sniff walk (evening, 20-30 min) | 20-30 min | Excellent for mental exercise | Free |
| Food puzzle for every meal (zero bowls) | 2 min to fill | Good — 15-20 min of mental work per meal | $10-$30 per puzzle |
| Dog sport class (1x/week) | 1-2 hours on weekends | Excellent — agility, nose work, or obedience tires brain + body | $100-$200 per 6-week session |
| Treadmill training (supervised only) | 15-20 min | Good for days when outdoor exercise is impossible (storms, extreme heat) | Dog treadmill: $500-$2,000; human treadmill used SUPERVISED: free if you own one |
