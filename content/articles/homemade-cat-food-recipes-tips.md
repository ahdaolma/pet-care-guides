## 10 Tips for Making Safe, Balanced Homemade Cat Food

![Homemade cat food tips](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Ftse4.mm.bing.net%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.plf7LdP4Mx6TV-aUMiiZxAHaHa%3Fr%3D0%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=d6fa67af5f5596d752d3536dfe11e2017ddcb6e952714cc87b45d6ba4911afae&ipo=images)

### 1. Do Not Use a Random Internet Recipe — 99% Are Nutritionally Inadequate

A 2019 JAVMA study analyzed 114 online homemade cat food recipes. Only 1 (0.9%) was nutritionally adequate. The most common missing nutrients: taurine, choline, iron, zinc, and vitamin E. A cat on a deficient diet looks healthy for months or years — then develops irreversible blindness, heart failure, or fractures. Use BalanceIT.com (free recipe generator by UC Davis veterinary nutritionists with their supplement powder) or consult a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (ACVN diplomate). Do not wing it.

### 2. Taurine Must Be Added — Cooking Destroys 50-75% of It

Cats cannot synthesize taurine. Raw heart is the best natural source; cooking destroys most of it. If you cook the food, taurine supplementation is mandatory. Dose: approximately 250-500 mg taurine powder per pound of food. Taurine is water-soluble — the excess is excreted, so slightly more is safe. Taurine deficiency causes: irreversible blindness (retinal degeneration, 12-18 months) and fatal heart failure (dilated cardiomyopathy, 18-36 months).

### 3. Liver Is Mandatory — But No More Than 5% of the Diet

Liver provides pre-formed vitamin A (cats cannot convert beta-carotene from plants), iron, copper, and B vitamins. Without liver, the diet is severely deficient. But liver is also toxically high in vitamin A — exceeding 5% of the total diet by weight causes hypervitaminosis A, which fuses vertebrae and cripples the cat permanently. Target: 1.6-1.8 oz of liver per 2.2 lbs of total food. Use a kitchen scale — do not eyeball it.

### 4. Calcium Must Be Balanced with Phosphorus — Meat Alone Causes Bone Disease

Pure muscle meat has a calcium-to-phosphorus ratio of roughly 1:15. The target is 1.1-1.4:1. Without bone or calcium supplementation, excess phosphorus leaches calcium from the cat's bones, causing spontaneous fractures of the pelvis and spine. Add ground raw bone (chicken wing tips — NEVER cooked bone, which splinters), eggshell powder (1/2 tsp per pound of meat, ground to a fine powder in a coffee grinder), or a calcium supplement per veterinary guidance.

### 5. Cats Are Obligate Carnivores — They Cannot Be Vegan or Vegetarian

Cats require nutrients found only in animal tissue: taurine, arachidonic acid, pre-formed vitamin A, and vitamin B12. They lack the enzymes to synthesize these from plant precursors. A vegan cat diet — even with synthetic supplementation — is experimental and high-risk. Multiple published case reports document vegan-fed cats developing dilated cardiomyopathy, blindness, and urinary tract disease. Do not impose your dietary ethics on an animal that evolved to eat meat exclusively.

### 6. Variety Matters — Do Not Feed Only Chicken Thighs Forever

Feeding a single protein source long-term creates nutritional gaps and increases the risk of developing food allergies (the immune system becomes sensitized to the over-represented protein). Rotate through at least 3 protein sources: chicken, turkey, pork, beef, rabbit, duck. Each has a different amino acid and fatty acid profile. Introducing variety early also prevents the "single-protein cat" that refuses all other foods — a dangerous situation if that protein becomes unavailable or the cat develops an allergy to it.

### 7. Never Feed Cooked Bones — They Shatter into Knife-Like Splinters

Raw bones are flexible and digestible. Cooked bones become brittle and splinter when chewed, creating sharp shards that perforate the esophagus, stomach, or intestines. This causes peritonitis (infection of the abdominal cavity), which is fatal without emergency surgery ($3,000-$6,000). If you feed bone, it must be RAW and GROUND. Weight-bearing bones of large animals (beef femur, pork shank) should be avoided even raw — they are too dense and crack teeth.

### 8. Raw Food Safety Is Real — Protect the Humans in Your Home

Cats have short, highly acidic digestive tracts adapted for raw meat — they are relatively resistant to foodborne pathogens. The humans in your household are not. Raw meat (especially poultry) carries Salmonella and Campylobacter. The cat sheds these bacteria in feces and saliva. Good practices: prepare raw food on a dedicated cutting board, sanitize all surfaces with a bleach solution after prep, wash hands for 30+ seconds, freeze meat for 2+ weeks before use (reduces — but does not eliminate — some parasites), and do NOT feed raw food if household members include: children under 5, adults over 65, pregnant women, or immunocompromised individuals.

### 9. Use BalanceIT.com as Your Starting Point

BalanceIT is a free online tool developed by board-certified veterinary nutritionists at UC Davis. You select your protein and carbohydrate sources, and it generates a recipe with precise dosing for their BalanceIT Feline supplement powder ($30-$45 per bag). This is the safest DIY approach short of a one-on-one nutritionist consultation. The supplement contains all micronutrients a homemade diet lacks: vitamins, minerals, taurine, and essential fatty acids. Do not substitute with generic supplements — the dosing is precise and formulation-specific.

### 10. Transition Slowly — Sudden Diet Changes Cause GI Upset

Switching from commercial to homemade food requires a 10-14 day transition. Start with 25% homemade / 75% old food for days 1-3, 50/50 for days 4-6, 75/25 for days 7-9, and 100% homemade from day 10. Monitor stool consistency throughout — loose stool means transition too fast. Cats imprint on food textures and may refuse unfamiliar formats — warming the food to body temperature and mixing with a small amount of warm water helps acceptance. A cat that refuses food for more than 24 hours is at risk for hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease), which is life-threatening — never "wait out" a hunger strike.
