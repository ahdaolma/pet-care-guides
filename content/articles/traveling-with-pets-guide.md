A 2025 survey by the American Pet Products Association found that 78% of pet owners travel with their pets at least once a year — up from 37% in 2015. The increase is driven by remote work flexibility and pet-friendly hotel policies. But traveling with a pet without preparation leads to lost pets, injuries, and denied boarding. This guide covers every mode of transport.

## Car Travel: The Crash Test Reality

An unrestrained 60-pound dog in a 35-mph crash becomes a 2,700-pound projectile. The Center for Pet Safety (CPS), an independent nonprofit that crash-tests pet restraints, has certified only a handful of products:

- **CPS Certified Harnesses:** Sleepypod Clickit Sport ($90), Sleepypod Clickit Terrain ($110). These are the ONLY harnesses that passed CPS independent crash testing as of 2025. They use a three-point connection system, not a single tether.
- **CPS Certified Carriers:** Sleepypod Mobile Pet Bed ($200), Gunner Kennel G1 ($500-700). These are crash-tested to the same standard as child safety seats.

**What does NOT work:** Standard dog seat belt attachments (single-point tethers that clip to a harness) — these snap in crashes. Wire crates — collapse on impact. Plastic airline crates not specifically crash-tested — shatter.

### The 2-Hour Rule

Stop every 2 hours for a 15-minute break. Dogs need to eliminate, drink water, and sniff (mental decompression). Highway rest stops are high-risk for diseases like parvovirus and leptospirosis — avoid areas where many dogs eliminate. Find a grassy area away from the main rest stop traffic.

### Temperature Safety

In 70°F weather, a car interior reaches 100°F within 20 minutes. At 85°F, it reaches 120°F in 10 minutes — fatal to dogs. If you stop, the dog comes with you. No exceptions. Cracking windows does nothing — multiple studies confirm interior temperature rises identically whether windows are cracked or closed.

## Air Travel: Know Before You Book

### In-Cabin (Pets Under 20 lbs with Carrier)

Most US airlines allow small pets in-cabin for $95-125 each way. The pet + carrier must fit under the seat in front of you (roughly 18" × 11" × 11" maximum, varies by airline). Book the pet reservation when you book your ticket — cabins have a pet limit (usually 4-6 per flight).

**Airline pet fees (2025, one-way, domestic):**
- Delta: $95 (US/Canada), $200 (international)
- American: $125
- United: $125
- Southwest: $95
- Alaska: $100
- JetBlue: $125
- Spirit: $125
- Frontier: $99

### Cargo (Pets Over 20 lbs)

Since 2020, most major US airlines have either severely restricted or eliminated pet cargo transport. American Airlines and Delta suspended checked pets as cargo in 2020 and have not fully restored the service. Alaska Airlines and Hawaiian Airlines still offer cargo for pets, but with temperature restrictions (no transport if temperature exceeds 85°F at any point in the journey or drops below 45°F).

The US Department of Transportation reported 21 animal deaths during air transport in 2023 (down from 39 in 2010). Brachycephalic breeds (French Bulldogs, Pugs, Boston Terriers) account for approximately half of all flight-related pet deaths due to respiratory compromise at altitude.

### International Travel

International pet travel requires: ISO microchip (15-digit, 134.2 kHz), rabies vaccination at least 21 days before travel, rabies titer test (FAVN) for rabies-free countries (results take 3-4 weeks), and an international health certificate from a USDA-accredited veterinarian endorsed by the APHIS Veterinary Services office. This process takes 4-6 months minimum. Start planning 6 months before your travel date.

### Emotional Support Animals (ESAs)

As of January 2021, the US Department of Transportation no longer requires airlines to accommodate ESAs. Only trained service dogs (task-trained to mitigate a disability) are guaranteed cabin access. ESAs are treated as regular pets and must fit in a carrier under the seat. Do not book a flight assuming your ESA letter grants cabin access — it does not.

## Veterinary Health Certificate

A Certificate of Veterinary Inspection (CVI, also called a health certificate) is required for:
- All interstate travel (dog shows, moving states)
- All international travel
- Some airlines even for domestic flights

The CVI must be issued within 10 days of travel (varies by state; some require 30 days). You need: proof of rabies vaccination, a physical exam documenting the pet is free of infectious disease, and the veterinarian's signature. Cost: $50-150. USDA endorsement (for international): additional $38-174 depending on the country.

## Packing List for Pet Travel

- Collar with ID tag (your cell phone number, not home — you are not home)
- Microchip registered with current contact info (check the microchip database before travel)
- 2+ days of food (more if driving long distances)
- Collapsible water bowl and bottled water (different water can cause GI upset)
- Medications + copy of prescription
- Vaccination records (digital copy on phone + paper copy)
- Recent photo of your pet (if they get lost)
- First aid kit: styptic powder (nail bleeding), vet wrap, Benadryl (1 mg/lb, vet-approved only), tweezers
- Crate or carrier (airline-compliant if flying, crash-tested if driving)
- Poop bags
- Favorite toy or blanket (familiar scent)

## Key Takeaway

Traveling with pets is about preparation, not improvisation. Book pet reservations with airlines and hotels at the same time you book your own. Use CPS-certified crash restraints in cars. Get the health certificate 10 days before travel. And never, ever leave a dog in a parked car.
