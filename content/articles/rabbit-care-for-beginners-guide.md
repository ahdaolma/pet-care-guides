## A Hutch in the Backyard Is a Prison — Rabbits Belong Indoors

![Rabbit care for beginners](https://external-content.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=https%3A%2F%2Fthfvnext.bing.com%2Fth%2Fid%2FOIP.hy8z1cCVG3QNnv_xU_dSwQHaMM%3Fcb%3Dthfvnextfalcon2%26pid%3DApi&f=1&ipt=3551601d05b9135c65322b2811e339b2a3777ca66f1fa5a9bf528eea0563c9df&ipo=images)

Rabbits are the third most surrendered animal to shelters after dogs and cats. The #1 reason: "The rabbit is boring — it just sits in its cage." A rabbit confined to a cage or outdoor hutch for 23+ hours per day IS boring — it is depressed, lonely, and physically deteriorating. In the wild, rabbits run 3-5 miles per day, dig complex warrens, and live in social groups of 10-50. A rabbit in a 4-square-foot pet store cage is living at approximately 1% of its natural activity level.

The House Rabbit Society (the largest rabbit welfare organization in the US) recommends: minimum 16 square feet of enclosure space (4×4 feet) for 1-2 rabbits, plus 4+ hours of supervised free-roam time daily in a rabbit-proofed room. Rabbits are litter-trainable, social, and bond deeply with their humans — but they are not low-maintenance starter pets. Their needs are comparable to a cat, with the medical fragility of an exotic animal.

This guide covers what rabbits actually need to thrive indoors as family members.

## Housing: 16+ Square Feet Minimum

| Option | Floor Space | Cost | Notes |
|--------|------------|------|-------|
| **Exercise pen (X-pen)** | 16-36 sq ft | $40-$80 | The most common indoor setup. A 48-inch tall metal pen configured in a 4×4 or 4×6 foot rectangle. Tall enough that most rabbits cannot jump over (some can — know your rabbit). |
| **Free-roam (rabbit-proofed room)** | Unlimited | Cost of rabbit-proofing | The ideal. A dedicated room or a rabbit-proofed section of a room. The rabbit has free access 24/7. Litter box, hide, toys, and food/water in the room. |
| **Dog crate + attached pen** | 16+ sq ft | $50-$80 for crate + pen | A 48-inch dog crate as a "home base" with an X-pen attached. The crate contains the litter box and hide; the pen provides exercise space. |
| **Pet store cage** | 2-5 sq ft | $50-$100 | CRUEL. Do not buy. These are travel carriers, not homes. |

**Flooring:** Rabbits do not have paw pads — their feet are covered in thick fur over thin skin. Wire-bottom cages cause ulcerative pododermatitis (sore hocks) — painful, infected pressure sores that require months of treatment and can lead to bone infection. Solid flooring is mandatory. Cover exercise pen floors with: low-pile area rugs, foam puzzle mats covered with fleece (rabbits may chew foam — cover completely), or linoleum remnants. Provide a soft resting surface (fleece blanket, cotton bath mat) in the hide area.

**Hide:** Every rabbit needs a covered space to retreat. A cardboard box with two entrance holes (rabbits need an escape route to feel safe), a wooden hide house, or a covered cat bed. The hide should be large enough for the rabbit to stretch out fully — rabbits sleep in a "loaf" position but should be able to flop on their side.

**Litter box:** A large cat litter box ($10-$15) or a concrete mixing tub from a hardware store ($10). Fill with paper-based, unscented litter (Yesterday's News, Carefresh). Do NOT use: clumping cat litter (rabbits may ingest it — clumps in the GI tract, causing fatal impaction), pine/cedar shavings (phenols cause liver damage), or corn/wheat-based litter (rabbits eat it, overloading on carbohydrates). Place hay IN or immediately adjacent to the litter box — rabbits eat and defecate simultaneously, and this habit makes litter training nearly automatic.

## Diet: 80% Hay, 10% Greens, 5% Pellets, 5% Treats

The rabbit digestive system is a continuously operating fermentation vat. It must never be empty — a rabbit that stops eating for 12+ hours is in a medical emergency (GI stasis — see below).

| Component | % of Diet | Examples | Daily Amount for 5-lb Rabbit |
|-----------|----------|----------|------------------------------|
| **Unlimited grass hay** | 80% | Timothy hay, orchard grass, meadow hay. Alfalfa hay ONLY for rabbits under 6 months (too high in calcium and protein for adults). | Unlimited — always available. A pile the size of the rabbit's body daily. |
| **Fresh leafy greens** | 10-15% | Romaine lettuce, red/green leaf lettuce, cilantro, parsley, dandelion greens, carrot tops, kale (in moderation — high calcium), spinach (in moderation — high oxalates) | 1-2 packed cups, divided into 2 meals |
| **High-quality pellets** | 5% | Oxbow Essentials Adult Rabbit Food, Sherwood Adult Rabbit Food. NO pellets with colorful bits, seeds, or dried fruit — those are junk food. | 1/8 to 1/4 cup (NOT free-fed) |
| **Treats** | <5% | Small piece of fruit (apple, banana, berries — 1 tablespoon max), carrot (1-2 baby carrots). These are candy to rabbits — high sugar, causes GI upset and obesity. | 1-2 tablespoons daily maximum |

**Toxic foods — NEVER:** Iceberg lettuce (contains lactucarium — causes diarrhea), avocado, chocolate, onions, garlic, potatoes, rhubarb, tomato leaves/stems, corn, seeds, nuts, human processed food.

**Water:** Fresh water in a heavy ceramic bowl (not a bottle — bottles restrict water intake and harbor bacteria in the spout). Change daily. Rabbits drink 50-150 mL per kg of body weight daily — a surprising amount. A rabbit that drinks noticeably less or more than usual needs a vet visit.

## Litter Training

Rabbits naturally designate a bathroom corner. In a new environment, place the litter box in the corner the rabbit has chosen — not where you want it. Once the rabbit reliably uses the box (1-2 weeks), you can move it gradually. Put hay in or directly next to the box — rabbits eat and poop simultaneously. Scoop the litter box daily. Full clean weekly. Un-neutered rabbits spray urine to mark territory — this behavior resolves 90%+ after spay/neuter.

## GI Stasis: The #1 Killer of Pet Rabbits

Gastrointestinal stasis is the slowing or complete cessation of gut motility. The rabbit's digestive system depends on constant movement — when movement stops, gas builds up, bacteria proliferates, toxins are absorbed into the bloodstream, and the rabbit dies within 24-72 hours without treatment.

**Causes:** Stress (loud noises, new environment, predator scent), pain (dental issues — rabbit teeth grow continuously), dehydration, low-fiber diet (not enough hay), sudden diet change, underlying illness.

**Signs:** Not eating (the most critical sign), no fecal pellets or small, misshapen pellets, hunched posture, teeth grinding (loud — sign of pain), cold ears, lethargy.

**Emergency action:** If your rabbit has not eaten for 12+ hours — this is a "go to the exotic vet NOW" situation. NOT "wait until morning." At home while preparing for transport: offer favorite fresh herbs (cilantro, parsley — the strong smell may stimulate appetite), gently massage the abdomen (if the rabbit tolerates it — do not force if painful), and keep the rabbit warm. Syringe feeding Critical Care (Oxbow, $15 per bag — every rabbit owner should keep this on hand) can buy time, but GI stasis requires veterinary treatment (fluids, pain medication, gut motility drugs, and sometimes X-rays to rule out blockage).

**Prevention:** Unlimited hay at all times, low-stress environment, annual dental check-ups (sedated exam with molar inspection — rabbit molars are impossible to see while awake), and never making sudden diet changes.

## Spay/Neuter: Health Essential, Not Optional

Unspayed female rabbits have an 80% incidence of uterine adenocarcinoma (cancer) by age 4. This is the single most preventable cause of death in female rabbits. Spaying eliminates this risk entirely. Neutered males are less aggressive, less territorial (no spraying), and can be bonded with other rabbits. Spay/neuter is safe when performed by an experienced rabbit-savvy veterinarian ($150-$400). Find a vet through the House Rabbit Society's recommended vet list — most general practice vets are not trained in rabbit surgery.
